### How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?
As Product Owner, I interpret user needs by focusing on what the user is trying to accomplish and why it matters, then translating that into clear, testable user stories. I make sure each story explains the goal (value) and includes acceptance criteria so the team knows what “done” actually looks like. User stories help because they keep requirements written from the user’s point of view and make it easier to prioritize work based on value. For SNHU Travel, that meant shaping stories around the core customer flow—browsing destinations, viewing details, selecting a package, and completing checkout—so we could deliver usable progress in small increments.

### How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?
I prefer building in small, reviewable increments instead of trying to design everything perfectly up front. Agile helps me do that by keeping work organized in a prioritized backlog and using sprints to stay focused on a realistic goal. Going forward, I want to keep using backlog refinement, sprint planning, sprint reviews, and retrospectives because they help me adjust quickly when priorities change. I also want to keep the habit of writing clear acceptance criteria, using a simple “Definition of Done,” and timeboxing refinement so planning stays useful without turning into scope creep.

### What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?
To me, being a good team member means being dependable, communicating clearly, and caring about the success of the whole team—not just your own tasks. As a Product Owner, that also means respecting the team’s time by keeping priorities clear, answering questions quickly, and making sure requirements aren’t vague. A good teammate is open to feedback, raises risks early, and helps keep work moving by reducing confusion and supporting shared goals.
